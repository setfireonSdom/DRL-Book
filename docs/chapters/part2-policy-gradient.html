9  Policy Gradient Methods

So far, the deep reinforcement learning methods in this book have followed the value-based path. We started from tabular Q-learning, moved to function approximation, and then studied DQN as a deep version of value-based control.

That path is powerful, but it is not the only one.

Policy gradient methods take a different approach. Instead of learning action values and then selecting actions indirectly, they optimize the policy itself. This shift changes the mathematical object we train, the kinds of action spaces we can handle naturally, and the kinds of stability problems we encounter.

9.1 Why Learn the Policy Directly?

In value-based methods, the policy is derived from value estimates. For example, in Q-learning or DQN, we estimate \(Q(s, a)\) and then choose actions by taking an argmax or by adding exploration.

This works well in many discrete-action problems, but it has limitations.

First, the argmax operation becomes awkward in continuous action spaces. If actions are continuous, finding the maximizing action at every step may be difficult or expensive.

Second, some tasks benefit from learning stochastic policies directly. In uncertain environments, partial observability, competitive settings, or environments with multiple near-optimal behaviors, a stochastic policy may be more natural than a deterministic one.

Third, direct policy optimization avoids some of the indirectness of value-based control. Rather than estimating action values and then extracting behavior, policy gradient methods optimize behavior itself.

This leads to a new parameterization:

\[ \pi_\theta(a \mid s) \]

where \(\theta\) are the parameters of the policy network.

9.2 The Objective of Policy Gradient Methods

The goal is to maximize expected return under the policy. At a high level, we may write the objective as:

\[ J(\theta) \]

where \(J\) measures how good the policy is in expectation.

The central question is:

How do we compute a gradient that tells us how to improve the policy parameters?

This is where the policy gradient theorem enters. The theorem gives a way to estimate \(\nabla_\theta J(\theta)\) using sampled trajectories, without needing to differentiate through the environment dynamics directly.

The deep idea is that if an action led to better-than-expected outcomes, then the policy should become more likely to take that action in similar states. If an action led to worse-than-expected outcomes, the policy should become less likely to take it.

9.3 The Log-Probability Trick

One of the most important identities behind policy gradients is:

\[ \nabla_\theta \pi_\theta(a \mid s) = \pi_\theta(a \mid s)\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a \mid s) \]

This may look like a technical step, but it is what makes policy optimization practical.

Using this identity, we can derive estimators that scale the gradient of the log-probability of an action by a signal that measures how good that action turned out to be.

This leads to the core intuition:

Increase the probability of actions that lead to high return, and decrease the probability of actions that lead to low return.

9.4 REINFORCE

The classic Monte Carlo policy gradient algorithm is REINFORCE. Its update has the form:

\[ \theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha G_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t \mid S_t) \]

Here, \(G_t\) is the return observed after taking action \(A_t\) in state \(S_t\).

This update says:

  1. If the return after an action is high, increase the log-probability of that action.
  2. If the return is low, reduce its relative probability.

REINFORCE is appealing because it is conceptually direct. It does not require a value function in its simplest form. It uses sampled returns to improve the policy directly.

This makes REINFORCE an important baseline for understanding the entire policy optimization family.

9.5 Why REINFORCE Is Noisy

REINFORCE is elegant, but it suffers from a serious practical problem:

Its gradient estimates can have very high variance.

There are several reasons for this:

  1. Returns can be noisy across episodes.
  2. Credit assignment is difficult when rewards are delayed.
  3. Full-trajectory returns may fluctuate heavily even when the policy changes only slightly.

As a result, learning can be unstable or slow. The algorithm points in the right direction on average, but the individual gradient estimates can be extremely noisy.

This is one reason why pure Monte Carlo policy gradients are often more useful as teaching tools or baseline methods than as the final answer in serious reinforcement learning systems.

9.6 Baselines and Variance Reduction

One of the most important ideas in policy gradient methods is the use of a baseline.

Instead of weighting the gradient by the raw return \(G_t\), we subtract a baseline \(b(s)\):

\[ \theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha \left(G_t - b(S_t)\right)\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t \mid S_t) \]

If chosen properly, this does not change the expected gradient, but it can reduce variance substantially.

The intuition is simple. We do not only want to know whether a return was large in absolute terms. We want to know whether it was better or worse than some reasonable expectation.

That “better than expected” idea later becomes the advantage function, which is central to actor-critic methods.

9.7 Policy Networks in Practice

In deep reinforcement learning, the policy is often represented by a neural network.

For discrete actions, the network may output logits or probabilities over actions. For continuous actions, it may output the parameters of a probability distribution, such as a Gaussian mean and variance.

The training signal then comes from log-probabilities of sampled actions multiplied by returns, advantages, or related estimates.

Older implementations often present this structure in a compact form:

  1. The network produces action probabilities.
  2. An action is sampled from that distribution.
  3. The log-probability of the sampled action is recorded.
  4. After rewards are collected, the policy parameters are updated using a weighted log-probability objective.

That structure remains conceptually correct even when the surrounding software stack is old.

In the maintained examples for this project, the cleanest reference for this idea is now a self-implemented PyTorch REINFORCE example for CartPole:

  1. examples/deep_rl/reinforce_cartpole.py

It records action log-probabilities during an episode, computes discounted returns afterward, and then performs a return-weighted policy update. That example is intentionally kept as the simplest policy-gradient reference in the codebase.

That role should be read carefully. reinforce_cartpole.py is not presented here as the strongest maintained policy optimization baseline in the project. It is presented as the clearest local implementation of the policy-gradient idea itself. If the question is “what does a return-weighted log-probability update look like in code?”, this is the right file to read first.

9.8 Strengths of Policy Gradient Methods

Policy gradient methods have several important strengths.

First, they support stochastic policies naturally.

Second, they handle continuous action spaces more gracefully than max-based value methods.

Third, they optimize the policy directly rather than relying on a value-to-policy extraction step.

Fourth, they provide a clean foundation for many later methods, including actor-critic algorithms, PPO, TRPO, and other modern policy optimization methods.

These strengths help explain why policy-based deep reinforcement learning became such a major branch of the field.

In the current code for this book, pure policy gradient is represented mainly by REINFORCE. More advanced policy optimization examples such as PPO are maintained separately and, at present, are backed by a mature third-party implementation rather than a from-scratch local reimplementation. That decision is practical: for this project, a stable and validated training baseline matters more than insisting that every policy method be hand-written first.

This means the codebase currently separates two goals that are often conflated:

  1. a transparent local implementation that makes the principle easy to inspect,
  2. and a stronger maintained baseline that is more reliable to run.

For policy gradients in this project, REINFORCE mainly serves the first role.

9.9 Weaknesses of Pure Policy Gradients

At the same time, pure policy gradient methods also have clear weaknesses.

  1. Gradient estimates can have high variance.
  2. Learning may require many samples.
  3. Credit assignment can still be difficult in long-horizon tasks.
  4. Without value estimation, the algorithm may use data inefficiently.

These weaknesses point directly toward the next major idea:

Combine policy learning with value estimation.

That hybrid approach leads to actor-critic methods.

9.10 Why Policy Gradients Matter

Policy gradients matter because they expand the reinforcement learning toolbox beyond greedy value maximization. They show that decision-making policies can be parameterized and optimized directly, which becomes especially important in continuous control and large-scale deep reinforcement learning.

They also introduce several ideas that will remain central:

  1. Log-probability objectives.
  2. Return-weighted policy updates.
  3. Baselines for variance reduction.
  4. The separation between policy optimization and value estimation.

The next chapter builds on these ideas by combining a policy learner with a value estimator in the actor-critic framework.
