11  Actor-Critic Methods

Policy gradient methods optimize the policy directly, which gives them flexibility and conceptual clarity. But pure policy gradients often suffer from high variance and poor sample efficiency. Value-based methods, on the other hand, use bootstrapping to learn more efficiently, but they may be less natural in continuous action settings and can be unstable in other ways.

Actor-critic methods combine the strengths of both approaches.

This is one of the most important design patterns in modern reinforcement learning.

11.1 The Main Idea

An actor-critic method has two components:

  1. The actor, which represents the policy.
  2. The critic, which estimates how good states, actions, or decisions are.

The actor decides what to do. The critic evaluates those decisions and provides a learning signal.

This division of labor is powerful because it separates two difficult problems:

  1. How to choose actions.
  2. How to judge whether those actions were good.

In deep reinforcement learning, both actor and critic are often neural networks, though their exact forms vary across algorithms.

11.2 Why Add a Critic?

The main reason to add a critic is variance reduction and improved learning efficiency.

Instead of waiting for a full Monte Carlo return as in REINFORCE, the critic can provide a learned estimate of future return. That estimate becomes a stronger, more immediate training signal for the actor.

In its simplest form, the actor update may look like:

\[ \theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha \hat{A}_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(A_t \mid S_t) \]

where \(\hat{A}_t\) is an estimate of the advantage of the selected action.

This already shows the core relationship:

The actor changes the policy, and the critic supplies the signal that says whether the chosen action was better or worse than expected.

11.3 Why Actor-Critic Is Easier to Misread Than REINFORCE

For beginners, actor-critic often feels more confusing than either Q-learning or REINFORCE because it mixes two learning stories at once.

  1. The actor is trying to improve behavior.
  2. The critic is trying to estimate a value signal.
  3. Each one depends on the other.

That means a reader can no longer ask only “what is the update?” The better question is:

Which network is learning what, and what signal passes between them?

If that interface is not clear, actor-critic quickly turns into a blur of names.

11.4 The Advantage Function

The advantage function is one of the central ideas in actor-critic methods. It is defined as:

\[ A^\pi(s, a) = Q^\pi(s, a) - V^\pi(s) \]

It measures how much better or worse an action is relative to the state’s average value under the current policy.

This is useful because raw returns alone do not tell us whether an action was surprisingly good. A large return may simply reflect that the state itself was favorable. The advantage function tries to isolate the contribution of the action relative to the baseline expectation.

This is precisely the kind of signal that helps reduce variance in policy updates.

11.5 Temporal-Difference Critics

Many actor-critic methods use TD learning for the critic. For example, the critic may update a state-value estimate using:

\[ V(S_t) \leftarrow V(S_t) + \alpha \left(R_{t+1} + \gamma V(S_{t+1}) - V(S_t)\right) \]

The TD error

\[ \delta_t = R_{t+1} + \gamma V(S_{t+1}) - V(S_t) \]

often serves a dual role:

  1. It updates the critic.
  2. It acts as a policy learning signal for the actor.

This is elegant because the same one-step surprise signal helps both components learn together.

For beginners, the TD error is often the first place where actor-critic becomes concrete.

  1. If \(\delta_t\) is positive, the outcome was better than the critic expected.
  2. If \(\delta_t\) is negative, the outcome was worse than the critic expected.
  3. The critic uses that signal to fix its value estimate.
  4. The actor uses that signal to decide whether to reinforce or weaken the chosen action.

That is the teaching core of actor-critic in one place: one signal, two jobs.

11.6 A Basic Actor-Critic Update

A simple actor-critic algorithm might follow this loop:

  1. Observe state \(S_t\).
  2. Sample action \(A_t\) from the actor.
  3. Receive reward \(R_{t+1}\) and next state \(S_{t+1}\).
  4. Compute a TD-style target for the critic.
  5. Compute a TD error or advantage estimate.
  6. Update the critic toward the target.
  7. Update the actor using the advantage-weighted log-probability gradient.

This structure is one of the cleanest examples of how reinforcement learning combines prediction and control in a single learning system.

11.7 A Beginner Mental Model

If REINFORCE says:

“This action led to good return, so increase its probability,”

then actor-critic says:

“This action did better or worse than the critic expected, so adjust the policy accordingly.”

That difference matters because it explains why actor-critic is usually less noisy than pure REINFORCE. The critic provides a learned baseline instead of forcing the actor to rely only on raw full-episode returns.

Another way to say it is:

  1. REINFORCE waits for the whole story.
  2. Actor-critic listens to a running reviewer.

That reviewer can be helpful or harmful depending on whether the critic is learning well.

11.8 Synchronous and Asynchronous Variants

Historically, actor-critic ideas appeared in several forms, including synchronous and asynchronous training.

One notable family was A3C (Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic), where multiple workers interacted with environments in parallel and updated shared global parameters. This approach helped reduce correlation in experience and made training more efficient at the time.

Older repositories often contain A3C-style implementations with threaded workers, local copies of policy and value networks, and periodic synchronization. Even though the software style may look dated, the conceptual lesson remains important:

Parallel data collection and advantage-based policy updates can make actor-critic methods much more practical.

Later systems often moved toward synchronous batched training, but the actor-critic principle remained intact.

11.9 A2C and the Simplification of A3C

A2C (Advantage Actor-Critic) is often described as a synchronous counterpart to A3C. Instead of many asynchronous workers updating parameters independently, data is often collected in parallel and then used in more coordinated batch updates.

This simplification can make implementations easier to reason about and debug. It also aligns better with modern accelerator-based training workflows.

The deeper point is not the naming difference between A2C and A3C. The deeper point is that both rely on the same core structure:

  1. A policy network proposes actions.
  2. A value estimator supplies a baseline or advantage signal.
  3. The actor is updated in the direction of actions that appear better than expected.

11.10 Why Actor-Critic Became Central

Actor-critic methods became central because they occupy a strong middle ground.

Compared with pure policy gradients, they usually reduce variance and improve sample efficiency.

Compared with purely value-based methods, they often handle stochastic or continuous policies more naturally.

Compared with pure Monte Carlo methods, they can learn incrementally through bootstrapping.

This balance explains why so many modern deep reinforcement learning algorithms are actor-critic variants in one form or another.

It also explains why actor-critic is harder to implement robustly than its pseudocode may suggest. Once a policy update depends on a learned value signal, the system becomes a coupled optimization problem. The actor depends on the critic for guidance, and the critic depends on data generated by the actor. That feedback loop is powerful, but it is also where many implementation failures come from.

11.11 Remaining Challenges

Actor-critic methods are powerful, but they are not automatically stable.

They inherit complexity from both sides:

  1. The actor can drift too quickly.
  2. The critic can become inaccurate or biased.
  3. Bootstrapping can still create instability.
  4. Off-policy variants can become especially delicate.

In practice, a weak critic can damage the actor badly, because the policy update depends on the critic’s signal. This creates a feedback loop similar in spirit to the issues seen in deep value learning.

That is why later actor-critic methods often include additional design choices such as clipping, trust-region style constraints, entropy regularization, target networks, replay buffers, or delayed updates.

11.12 From Actor-Critic to Modern DRL

Actor-critic is less a single algorithm than a broad framework. Many influential deep reinforcement learning methods can be understood as actor-critic descendants or refinements.

Examples include:

  1. A2C and A3C.
  2. DDPG for continuous control.
  3. TD3 as a refinement of deterministic actor-critic ideas.
  4. SAC as an entropy-regularized actor-critic method.
  5. PPO as a practical policy optimization method built around actor-critic structure.

This is why actor-critic deserves special attention. It is one of the most fertile templates in the entire field.

In the maintained code for this book, actor-critic ideas are currently represented in two different ways.

First, the simplest policy-gradient baseline remains a local self-implementation:

  1. examples/deep_rl/reinforce_cartpole.py

Second, the main actor-critic training path is currently represented by stable library-backed LunarLander examples:

  1. examples/deep_rl/actor_critic_lunarlander.py
  2. examples/deep_rl/a2c_lunarlander.py
  3. examples/deep_rl/ppo_lunarlander.py

These files use Stable-Baselines3 rather than local from-scratch implementations. That choice is deliberate. In this project, the actor-critic family proved much more sensitive to implementation details than the value-based DQN family, so the maintained baseline now prioritizes validated training behavior over hand-written minimalism.

From a conceptual perspective, this is still appropriate. The point of this chapter is to understand the actor-critic pattern: policy updates guided by a learned value-based signal. The specific software boundary between “written locally” and “delegated to a stable library” is secondary to that core idea.

Still, the implementation boundary should be stated explicitly because it affects how the code should be used:

  1. reinforce_cartpole.py is the local conceptual baseline for direct policy gradients.
  2. ppo_lunarlander.py is currently the strongest maintained actor-critic-family baseline in the repository.
  3. actor_critic_lunarlander.py and a2c_lunarlander.py preserve the algorithmic positions in the codebase, but they should be read with the same practical caution used elsewhere in this book: an algorithm name in the repository is not automatically a claim that every variant is equally validated.

11.13 How to Read the Maintained Code Without Getting Lost

The maintained actor-critic-facing files in this repository are not all serving the same teaching role, so beginners should read them differently.

11.13.1 First read reinforce_cartpole.py

That file is still the best place to understand:

  1. what a policy network looks like,
  2. what a log-probability update looks like,
  3. how rewards turn into a policy-learning signal.

It is not actor-critic yet, but it makes the actor side legible.

11.13.2 Then read the SB3-backed LunarLander wrappers

Files such as actor_critic_lunarlander.py and a2c_lunarlander.py are much thinner.

They mainly show:

  1. which algorithm class is being used,
  2. what high-level hyperparameters are chosen,
  3. and how training and evaluation are wired into the repository.

For example, the key lines in a2c_lunarlander.py are not a hand-written actor update. They are the configuration boundary:

result = train_sb3_lunarlander(
    algo_name="a2c_lunarlander",
    algo_class=A2C,
    total_timesteps=config.total_timesteps,
    ...
)

So the right beginner reading is:

  1. use the local REINFORCE file to understand the actor side,
  2. use this chapter to understand what the critic adds,
  3. use the SB3-backed files to see how the repository packages a practical actor-critic baseline.

That order is much less confusing than expecting the wrapper files themselves to teach actor-critic from first principles.

11.14 Common Beginner Confusions About Actor-Critic

11.14.1 “Is actor-critic just REINFORCE plus a value network?”

At a high level, that is a useful first approximation. The critic provides a learned value-based signal that replaces or improves upon raw return-only policy updates. But the resulting training dynamics are more coupled than that phrase suggests.

11.14.2 “Why can a bad critic hurt the actor so much?”

Because the actor is trusting the critic’s signal. If the critic says a weak action was better than expected, the policy may become more likely to repeat it. So actor-critic stability depends heavily on critic quality.

11.14.3 “Why are the local code examples thinner here than in DQN?”

Because in this project, the actor-critic family was more sensitive to engineering details. The repository now prefers stable maintained baselines over pretending that every algorithm has an equally clean from-scratch local implementation.
