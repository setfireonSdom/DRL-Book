4  Markov Decision Processes

The agent-environment framework gives us intuition, but we still need a mathematical structure that makes reinforcement learning precise. The standard framework is the Markov decision process (MDP). It provides a clean way to describe states, actions, rewards, transitions, and long-term objectives.

Many reinforcement learning algorithms can be understood as different ways of solving, approximating, or learning within an MDP.

4.1 The Markov Property

The defining assumption behind an MDP is the Markov property. Informally, it says that the future depends on the present state, not on the full past history, once the present state is known.

In words:

The current state contains all information needed for predicting the next step.

This does not mean the past is irrelevant in general. It means the state has already summarized the relevant parts of the past. If the state representation fails to do that, then the process is not fully Markov from the agent’s point of view.

This point is important in practice. Many real environments are only approximately Markov. Partial observability, noisy sensors, hidden variables, and missing context can all break the assumption. Deep reinforcement learning often deals with this by learning richer representations, stacking observations, or using recurrent models.

If you remember one thing from this section, it can be stated very simply:

A good state is supposed to summarize the past well enough that the agent can decide what matters next.

4.2 The MDP Components

An MDP is often described by the tuple:

\[ (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R, \gamma) \]

where:

  1. \(\mathcal{S}\) is the set of states.
  2. \(\mathcal{A}\) is the set of actions.
  3. \(P\) is the transition dynamics.
  4. \(R\) is the reward function.
  5. \(\gamma\) is the discount factor.

The transition dynamics tell us how the environment changes after an action. More formally, we may write:

\[ P(s', r \mid s, a) \]

which means the probability of moving to state \(s'\) and receiving reward \(r\) after taking action \(a\) in state \(s\).

This form captures both uncertainty in the next state and uncertainty in the reward.

The abstraction may seem very mathematical at first, but in code it usually appears as a small number of concrete data structures.

In the maintained tabular examples for this project, the cleanest place to see this is:

  1. examples/tabular/gridworld.py

That file makes the MDP components explicit:

  1. States are grid coordinates such as (row, col).
  2. Actions are symbols such as "U", "D", "L", and "R".
  3. Rewards are stored in dictionaries keyed by states.
  4. Available actions are stored in dictionaries keyed by states.
  5. The windy variant stores transition probabilities explicitly in transition_probs.

This is a useful point pedagogically. Before reinforcement learning becomes deep or statistical, an MDP is simply a structured description of what can happen next and what reward is attached to it.

4.3 A Beginner Mental Model for MDPs

Many beginners first meet MDP notation and feel that the subject suddenly became more abstract than the bandit chapter. A helpful way to resist that feeling is to translate the tuple back into plain language:

  1. What situations can I be in?
  2. What choices do I have there?
  3. What tends to happen after each choice?
  4. What reward do I receive?
  5. How much do I care about the future?

That is all the MDP formalism is trying to pin down. The notation is important, but the purpose of the notation is simply to make those five questions precise.

In code, the same idea is much less intimidating:

rewards = {(0, 3): 1.0, (1, 3): -1.0}
actions = {
    (0, 0): ("D", "R"),
    ...
}

That is why gridworld.py is such a good companion file. It turns the abstract tuple into concrete Python objects.

4.4 Policies Inside an MDP

Once an MDP is defined, the agent still needs a policy. A policy determines how actions are selected in each state. If \(\pi\) is a policy, then:

\[ \pi(a \mid s) \]

is the probability that the agent chooses action \(a\) in state \(s\).

The policy and the environment together induce a distribution over trajectories. That is why reinforcement learning is fundamentally probabilistic even when the environment or policy is deterministic in special cases.

4.5 Returns and Objectives

The main objective in an MDP is usually to maximize expected return. Starting at time \(t\), the discounted return is:

\[ G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k R_{t+k+1} \]

This equation formalizes the idea that current actions should be judged by their future consequences, not only by immediate rewards.

There are several reasons discounting is useful:

  1. It gives more weight to near-term rewards.
  2. It often makes infinite-horizon sums well-defined.
  3. It can reduce sensitivity to very distant uncertainty.

In episodic tasks, the return may terminate naturally at the end of an episode. In continuing tasks, discounting often plays a larger role.

In plain language, return is just the answer to this question:

How good was this situation when future consequences are included, not just the next reward?

4.6 State-Value and Action-Value Functions

Value functions describe expected future return under a policy. They translate the long-term objective into quantities that can be estimated and improved.

The state-value function is:

\[ V^\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi [G_t \mid S_t = s] \]

It tells us how good it is to be in state \(s\) if we continue to follow policy \(\pi\).

The action-value function is:

\[ Q^\pi(s, a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi [G_t \mid S_t = s, A_t = a] \]

It tells us how good it is to take action \(a\) in state \(s\) and then follow policy \(\pi\).

These functions are central because they support both evaluation and control. If we know which states and actions have high long-term value, we can improve decisions accordingly.

4.7 Bellman Equations

One of the most important ideas in reinforcement learning is that value functions satisfy recursive relationships. This is the core insight behind the Bellman equations.

For the state-value function:

\[ V^\pi(s) = \sum_a \pi(a \mid s) \sum_{s', r} P(s', r \mid s, a) \left[r + \gamma V^\pi(s')\right] \]

This equation says that the value of a state equals the expected immediate reward plus the discounted value of the next state.

Similarly, the action-value function satisfies:

\[ Q^\pi(s, a) = \sum_{s', r} P(s', r \mid s, a) \left[r + \gamma \sum_{a'} \pi(a' \mid s') Q^\pi(s', a')\right] \]

These recursive forms are powerful because they allow value estimation to be built from local consistency conditions rather than from full trajectory enumeration.

This is often the first intimidating equation in reinforcement learning, but the idea behind it is much smaller than the notation:

The value now should match immediate reward plus the value of what happens next.

In this repository, that recursive structure is not hidden behind a framework. It is written out directly in the tabular examples. The most transparent places to inspect are:

  1. examples/tabular/policy_evaluation.py
  2. examples/tabular/value_iteration.py

Those files are worth reading slowly. The Bellman equations are not only identities on paper. In code, they become loops over states, actions, and possible next states. The theory says a value should satisfy a recursive consistency relation. The implementation turns that statement into repeated sweeps until the estimates stop moving very much.

4.8 What a Beginner Should Look For in the Code

When reading the tabular files connected to this chapter, do not try to memorize every loop. Instead, ask:

  1. Where are the states represented?
  2. Where are the actions represented?
  3. Where does the reward enter the computation?
  4. Where does the next-state value appear?

If those four pieces are visible, then the Bellman equation has already become concrete.

4.9 Optimal Value Functions

So far, the value functions have been defined relative to a fixed policy \(\pi\). But reinforcement learning usually aims to find the best possible policy.

The optimal state-value function is:

\[ V^*(s) = \max_\pi V^\pi(s) \]

and the optimal action-value function is:

\[ Q^*(s, a) = \max_\pi Q^\pi(s, a) \]

These optimal value functions also satisfy Bellman equations. For example:

\[ V^*(s) = \max_a \sum_{s', r} P(s', r \mid s, a)\left[r + \gamma V^*(s')\right] \]

and

\[ Q^*(s, a) = \sum_{s', r} P(s', r \mid s, a)\left[r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q^*(s', a')\right] \]

These equations replace the policy expectation with a maximization over actions. That single change leads directly to many control algorithms, including Q-learning and DQN.

That is the turning point from “how good is this policy?” to “what is the best action to aim for here?”

That single change is easy to underestimate. In fact, it is one of the most important transitions in the whole book. When a policy expectation is replaced by a maximization, the problem changes from evaluating a given behavior to searching for the best behavior.

You can already see this shift in the maintained code before any learning from data begins:

  1. examples/tabular/policy_iteration.py
  2. examples/tabular/value_iteration.py

Then later, in the learning chapters, the same structural change reappears in:

  1. examples/tabular/q_learning.py
  2. examples/deep_rl/dqn_cartpole.py

So the Bellman optimality equation should not be read here as an isolated theorem. It is the mathematical ancestor of the greedy bootstrap target used much later in Q-learning and DQN.

4.10 Why MDPs Matter for Deep Reinforcement Learning

At first glance, deep reinforcement learning may seem far away from the clean equations in this chapter. In practice, however, the deep part mostly changes how value functions, policies, or models are represented. It does not change the core logic of the problem.

DQN still tries to approximate an action-value function. Policy gradient methods still optimize a policy in an MDP. Actor-critic methods still rely on value estimates defined through expected return. Even advanced algorithms remain built on the same foundational objects.

This is why MDPs matter. They provide the conceptual skeleton of the entire field.

For this reason, it is worth returning to the MDP language repeatedly while reading later chapters. Even when the code starts to use replay buffers, neural networks, target networks, or third-party training libraries, the foundational questions do not change:

  1. What is the state?
  2. What are the available actions?
  3. What is the reward signal?
  4. What return is being optimized?
  5. Where is the Bellman or policy objective appearing in the update?

If those questions are kept visible, the later algorithms stay interpretable. If they are forgotten, deep reinforcement learning can easily collapse into a collection of names and engineering recipes.

4.11 Common Beginner Confusions About MDPs

4.11.1 “Does an MDP mean the environment is simple?”

No. It only means the problem is being described in a structured way. An MDP can still be large, stochastic, partially observed from the agent’s point of view, or hard to solve.

4.11.2 “Why do we need this formalism before learning algorithms?”

Because later algorithms are all trying to estimate or optimize something defined inside this framework. Without the MDP view, it becomes much harder to tell what a policy, value function, or Bellman update is actually about.
